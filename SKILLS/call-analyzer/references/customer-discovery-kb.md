## 1. The Core Problem

**The fundamental trap:** We go into conversations wanting to be validated. Customers, wanting to be polite, give us what we want. We leave feeling great. We've learned nothing real.

> "Bad customer conversations aren't just useless. Worse, they convince you that you're on the right path. They give you a false positive which causes you to over-invest your cash, your time, and your team." — Fitzpatrick

**The two failure modes:**
- **False positives**: Collecting compliments and interpreting them as signal
- **False negatives**: Dismissing real pain because you asked the wrong question and got a lukewarm answer

**The root cause:** Asking about opinions, hypotheticals, and your idea — instead of asking about their actual life and past behavior.

## 2. The Mom Test: Three Fundamental Rules

The Mom Test produces questions even your mom can't lie to you about, because she never knows you have an idea.

### Rule 1: Talk about their life instead of your idea
- Never pitch your idea in a discovery conversation
- You find out if people care by *never mentioning it*
- Once you mention your idea, they shift into "help this person" mode and stop telling the truth

### Rule 2: Ask about specifics in the past instead of generics or opinions about the future
- Past behavior is data. Future intent is fiction.
- "Would you ever…" → worthless
- "When is the last time you…" → gold
- "What happened the last time X came up?" → gold

### Rule 3: Talk less, listen more
- The more you talk, the worse you're doing
- If someone starts a sentence and you interrupt to "fix" their understanding, you've lost the learning
- When you're talking, they're not revealing anything

## 3. Question Taxonomy

### ❌ Bad Questions (and why)

| Question | Problem |
|---|---|
| "Do you think it's a good idea?" | Invites opinion, not fact. "Only the market can tell if your idea is good." |
| "Would you buy a product that did X?" | Hypothetical future; answer is almost always yes |
| "How much would you pay for X?" | Same as above; number makes it feel rigorous but it's still fiction |
| "Would you ever use something like this?" | Fluff-inducing; "of course they might, someday" |
| "Do you like it?" | Invites compliments; data-free |
| "Would you pay X for Y?" | Future hypothetical with number added; still useless |

### ✅ Good Questions (and why)

| Question | Value |
|---|---|
| "Why do you bother?" | Gets to root motivation behind surface request |
| "What are the implications of that?" | Distinguishes "I'd pay to fix this" from "it's annoying but fine" |
| "Talk me through the last time that happened." | Specific past behavior; can't be wishy-washy |
| "Talk me through your workflow." | Reveals actual behavior, tools, pain points simultaneously |
| "What else have you tried?" | Reveals whether the problem is real (real problems get googled) |
| "How are you dealing with it now?" | Surfaces workaround + pricing anchor |
| "Where does the money come from?" | Critical in B2B to find actual budget holder |
| "Who else should I talk to?" | Good relationship signal + next leads |
| "Is there anything else I should have asked?" | Catches what you don't know you don't know |
| "What are your big goals/focuses right now?" | Reveals whether your problem is even in their top 3 |

## 4. Three Types of Bad Data

### 4a. Compliments
- Compliments are the fool's gold of customer learning: shiny, distracting, worthless
- **Deflect, don't absorb**. When you hear "I love it!" immediately redirect: "Thanks — how are you handling this right now?"
- Warning signs you're collecting compliments:
  - "That meeting went really well"
  - "We're getting a lot of positive feedback"
  - "Everybody I've talked to loves the idea"
  
### 4b. Fluff (Generics, Hypotheticals, Future Tense)
**Fluff triggers:** "I usually," "I always," "I would," "I might," "I could," "Would you ever"

**The most dangerous fluff:** "I would definitely buy that." — sounds concrete, is fiction.

**How to anchor fluff:**
1. Let them give the generic claim
2. Ask: "When's the last time that happened?"
3. Ask: "Can you talk me through it?"
4. Now you're working with real data

### 4c. Feature Requests / Ideas
- Feature requests reveal *symptoms*, not *causes*
- Write them down, don't obey them
- Always dig into the motivation: "Why do you want that? What would that let you do? How are you coping without it?"
- Undigested feature requests led Fitzpatrick's team to waste 3 months building a self-serve analytics dashboard that customers never used — because the real need was "send us a simple branded PDF every Friday"

## 5. Signs You Are Just Going Through the Motions

If you see any of these in a transcript, the conversation has been compromised:

- You're talking more than they are
- They are complimenting you or your idea
- You told them about your idea without knowing what happens next
- You have no notes
- You got an unexpected answer and it didn't change your thinking
- You weren't scared of any question you asked
- You aren't sure which big question you were trying to answer
- The meeting "went well" without any commitment or next step
- You left with warmth but no advancement

## 6. Commitment & Advancement

**The zombie lead problem:** Customers keep being friendly, keep taking meetings, keep saying nice things — but never cut a check. Caused by never asking for commitment.

**Commitment currencies:**
- **Time**: Clear next meeting with defined goals, trying a prototype for a week, filling in wireframe feedback
- **Reputation**: Intro to their boss, intro to peers, public testimonial
- **Money**: Pre-order, deposit, letter of intent

**The spectrum of meeting outcomes (worst → best):**
1. ❌ "That's so cool, I love it!" — pure compliment, zero data
2. ❌ "Looks great. Let me know when it launches." — compliment + stalling tactic
3. ⚠️ "There are a couple people I can intro you to when you're ready." — weak, fuzzy
4. ✅ "What are the next steps?" — advancement signal
5. ✅ "When can we start the trial?" — cost commitment (if trial has real cost)
6. ✅ "When can you come back to talk to the rest of the team?" — enterprise buying signal
7. ✅ "Can I buy the prototype?" — the best meeting ending possible

**The early evangelists / earlyvangelists (Blank's term):**
The ideal first customer has ALL FOUR:
1. Has the problem
2. Knows they have the problem
3. Has budget to solve it
4. Has already cobbled together their own workaround

Emotional intensity is the signal: "Yeah, that's a problem" vs. "THAT IS THE WORST PART OF MY WEEK."

## 7. Customer Segmentation

**Why broad segments kill learning:**
- You get 20 conversations and 20 different feature requests
- Everything "kind of works" for some segment
- You can't prove yourself right or wrong
- You're having one conversation each with 20 different customer types

**Customer slicing method:**
1. Start with a broad segment
2. Ask: "Within this group, who wants it most?"
3. Ask: "Does everyone in the group have this motivation, or only some?"
4. Ask: "Where can I find these specific people?"
5. Repeat until you have a **who-where pair** — a specific who AND a specific place to find them

**Signs your segment is too broad:** Inconsistent, contradictory feedback across conversations.

**Signs you have the right segment:** You stop hearing new information after 3–5 conversations.

## 8. Process: Before, During, After

### Before
- Define your top 3 learning goals with your team
- Face the scary questions (if your company fails, why? what must be true for it to succeed?)
- Do basic desk research first so you don't waste interview time on Googleable facts
- Know who you're talking to and what they care about

### During the call
- Frame the conversation (you're learning, not selling, not yet)
- Keep it casual — a casual chat beats a formal interview
- Ask Mom Test questions (past behavior, life context)
- Deflect compliments immediately
- Anchor fluff — "When was the last time that happened?"
- Dig beneath emotional signals and feature requests
- Take notes (exact quotes, signal symbols)
- Press for commitment and next steps before ending

### After
- Review notes with your whole team
- Transfer to permanent storage
- Update your beliefs and plans
- Define the next 3 big questions

## 9. B2B-Specific Principles (from The B2B Executive Playbook)

**The executive level is where strategy lives:**
- In B2B, the people who feel the pain often aren't the people who control budget
- Map the buying committee early: who owns budget, who can say no, who is the champion
- "Where does the money come from?" is a mandatory question in B2B

**Sustainable, predictable growth comes from:**
- Executive-level relationships, not just user-level adoption
- Understanding the customer's strategic priorities, not just their tactical pain
- Measuring success by the customer's outcomes, not your own product metrics
- Turning customers into advocates — advocacy is the strongest B2B signal you've found product-market fit

**The customer's job-to-be-done hierarchy in B2B:**
1. Strategic outcomes they're accountable for (what does their board measure?)
2. Operational challenges blocking those outcomes
3. Tactical problems day-to-day

Most vendors operate at level 3. Winning B2B relationships happen at level 1.

**Warning signals in B2B discovery:**
- Talking only to the champion, never to the economic buyer
- Solving operational pain without connecting it to strategic priority
- Moving to demo/pitch before understanding the buying process
- Missing key stakeholders in the buying committee

## 10. Quick Diagnosis Cheatsheet

### Red flags in a transcript (call needs improvement):
- [ ] Founder talks >50% of the time
- [ ] Hypothetical questions asked ("Would you ever…", "What would you pay…")
- [ ] Idea/solution described before problems explored
- [ ] Compliments accepted as validation ("Great, they love it!")
- [ ] Meeting ended without concrete next step or commitment
- [ ] Generic, non-specific answers accepted without anchoring
- [ ] No "scary" question asked
- [ ] Feature requests recorded without digging into motivation
- [ ] Emotional signals (frustration, excitement) not explored

### Green flags (good discovery):
- [ ] Customer talked 70%+ of the time
- [ ] All questions about past behavior, specific instances
- [ ] Unexpected answers caused the conversation to change direction
- [ ] At least one question that could disprove the business model
- [ ] Concrete commitment or clear next step agreed before ending
- [ ] Customer volunteered problems not in the question plan
- [ ] Specific workflow described with real constraints
- [ ] Economic buyer or buying process surfaced (B2B)
- [ ] Referrals or warm intros offered spontaneously
